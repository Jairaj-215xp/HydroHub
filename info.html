1. Atomic Physics & Isotopes

Hydrogen comes in three atomic forms (isotopes): Protium (Standard), Deuterium (Heavy), and Tritium (Radioactive). The only difference is the number of neutrons in the nucleus.

2. Extreme Energy Density

Hydrogen holds more energy per kilogram than any other fuel. 1 kg of Hydrogen contains the same energy as roughly 1 gallon (2.8 kg) of gasoline, or 100 kg of Lithium-Ion batteries!

3. The Fuel Cell Engine

Hydrogen Fuel Cell Electric Vehicles (FCEVs) don't burn hydrogen. They pass H₂ and O₂ through a Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM) to generate electricity, with pure water as the only exhaust.

4. Storage & Compression

Hydrogen gas is extremely diffuse. At normal atmospheric pressure (1 bar), 1kg takes up 11,000 Liters! We must compress it to 700 bar (10,000 psi) just to fit it inside a car's fuel tank.

1kg H₂

5. Hydrogen Safety & Leaks

Because hydrogen is 14x lighter than air, it is surprisingly safe in open environments. If a leak occurs, it rockets straight up and disperses, unlike propane which dangerously pools on the ground.

6. Gray vs Blue Hydrogen

Currently, 95% of hydrogen is "Gray", made by cracking natural gas (CH₄), which releases CO₂. "Blue" hydrogen captures that CO₂ and stores it underground.

9. Hydrogen Embrittlement

Because hydrogen is the smallest atom in the universe, it can physically slip between the iron atoms in a solid steel pipe, causing the steel to become brittle and crack.

10. The Invisible Flame

Hydrogen burns with a flame that is completely invisible in daylight, producing zero carbon emissions. You need a thermal camera just to see it.

11. Thermal Conductivity

Hydrogen gas conducts heat 7 times better than air. It is pumped into massive power plant generators just to rapidly pull the heat out of the core.

1000°C

12. Cryogenics & Spin States

Hydrogen only turns to liquid at a freezing -253°C. The protons can spin in the same direction (Ortho) or opposite (Para). Converting between them requires a catalyst to prevent the liquid from boiling.
